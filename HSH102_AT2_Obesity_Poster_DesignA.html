OBESITY IN AUSTRALIA

Epidemiology, Population Burden and Determinants of Disease
⚑ Public Health Significance

In 2024, overweight and obesity became Australia's #1 risk factor for total disease burden at 8.3%, surpassing tobacco use for the first time since national monitoring began (AIHW, 2024). The total economic cost was estimated at AUD $39 billion in 2019, equivalent to 1.9% of GDP, and is projected to exceed AUD $62 billion by 2030 (Obesity Evidence Hub, 2024). Obesity disproportionately affects Australians experiencing socioeconomic disadvantage, geographic remoteness and structural inequity (AIHW, 2024).

1. Disease Overview
What is Obesity?

Obesity is a chronic, complex, non-communicable disease (NCD) characterised by excessive adipose tissue accumulation that adversely impairs health. Defined as a Body Mass Index (BMI) of 30 kg/m² or above in adults (WHO, 2000), it is a multifactorial condition arising from the sustained interaction of biological, behavioural, environmental, social and commercial determinants, formally recognised as a disease state, not a lifestyle choice, by the Australian Government and international public health authorities (Commonwealth of Australia, 2022). Obesity substantially increases the risk of type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, musculoskeletal disorders, sleep apnoea, chronic kidney disease, and at least 13 forms of cancer (AIHW, 2024).

Determinant 1: Environmental
Environmental
The physical and food environment in which Australians live, work and travel profoundly shapes eating and physical activity patterns. Car-dependent suburban design, limited walking and cycling infrastructure, and unequal distribution of parks and recreational facilities reduce opportunities for incidental physical activity, particularly in low-income and outer suburban communities (National Obesity Strategy, 2022). Food environments are equally significant: access to affordable nutritious food is markedly limited in remote and low-socioeconomic areas, where fast food outlets and convenience stores outnumber fresh food retailers, creating so-called "food deserts" and "food swamps" (Swinburn et al., 1999; National Obesity Strategy, 2022). These obesogenic environments, physical surroundings that promote excess energy intake and limit energy expenditure, are a recognised structural driver of obesity that cannot be addressed through individual behaviour change alone.
Disease Classification
Disease type Non-communicable disease (NCD); chronic and multifactorial
Transmission Non-infectious; not transmissible person-to-person
Measurement BMI ≥30 kg/m² (adults); waist circumference at population level
Biological basis Dysregulation of energy balance; genetic, hormonal and metabolic factors interact with environmental exposures
Co-morbidities Type 2 diabetes, CVD, ≥13 cancers, musculoskeletal disorders, sleep apnoea, chronic kidney disease

Historical Context in Australia

Structured national surveillance began with the 1995 National Health Survey (NHS). Adult obesity prevalence has risen from 19% in 1995 to 32% in 2022, a 68% relative increase over 27 years (AIHW, 2024). Severe obesity (BMI ≥40) more than doubled from 2.2% in 2007-08 to 4.6% in 2022-23 (Obesity Evidence Hub, 2024). This trajectory reflects Australia's broader epidemiological transition, in which chronic NCDs have progressively displaced infectious diseases as the dominant cause of morbidity and mortality (AIHW, 2024).

3. Determinants of Disease Burden

Three determinants from distinct categories are presented below, reflecting the multifactorial and socially embedded nature of obesity in Australia (Commonwealth of Australia, 2022).

Determinant 2: Social
Social
A clear socioeconomic gradient shapes both the prevalence and burden of obesity across Australia. Australians in the lowest socioeconomic areas carry a disease burden from obesity 2.2 times greater than those in the highest socioeconomic areas (AIHW, 2021). Social determinants including income, educational attainment, employment status, housing quality and access to healthcare interact to constrain healthy choices and limit health-promoting behaviours. First Nations Australians experience significantly elevated rates: 74% of adults and 38% of children in 2018-19 (AIHW Indigenous HPF, 2024), rooted in the structural consequences of colonisation, intergenerational socioeconomic disadvantage, systemic racism, and inadequate access to culturally appropriate health services.
Determinant 3: Commercial
Commercial
The commercial food environment shapes population-level dietary behaviour through strategic marketing, pricing and distribution of energy-dense, nutrient-poor products. Between 2019 and 2023, advertising expenditure by Australia's food sector increased by 45%, with digital platforms enabling highly targeted advertising directed at children and young adults (Australian Government, 2024). No Australian Government regulation currently protects children from unhealthy food marketing; existing industry self-regulation codes have been independently assessed as inadequate (Obesity Evidence Hub, 2024).

Obesity rates have increased across all age groups since 1995, with the steepest rise in adults aged 45-74. A 9-percentage-point geographic gap exists between outer regional and remote communities versus major cities, reflecting differential access to healthy food, health services and recreational infrastructure (AIHW, 2024).
